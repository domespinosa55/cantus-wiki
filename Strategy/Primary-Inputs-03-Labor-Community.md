# Primary Inputs — Component 3: Labor, Workforce, and Community Embeddedness

**Series:** Primary Inputs Framework (3 of 3+)
**Practice:** Cantus Coordination
**Classification:** Internal Strategy Document

## Core Thesis

Community embeddedness is a primary input. Not an ESG framework or PR posture — a structural source of talent supply, deal flow, political alignment, and project credibility that competitors treating communities transactionally cannot replicate at any price.

Three observations drive the thesis:

1. **Labor is the binding constraint.** Federal CHIPS and IRA spending accelerated demand for skilled labor without addressing supply. Hyperscale operators face severe staffing constraints on technicians, electricians, and contractors.
2. **EDCs are the most underused deal flow network in U.S. industrial real estate.** National developers treat them transactionally. A developer with earned trust becomes the default first-call counterparty.
3. **Community impact determines political economy.** Whether a 500MW load gets utility commission approval, air permits, tax abatement, and legislative support are all functions of whether the developer is perceived as community-additive.

## Talent Pipeline Architecture

Four partnership categories serving different segments of the labor stack:

### R1 Research Universities
- Structured internship/co-op pipelines for engineering and operations students
- Sponsored research on power-heavy infrastructure development
- Executive education partnerships on reindustrialization curriculum
- Generates institutional credibility that compounds over time

### Community Colleges (Most Important Category)
- Hyperscale and advanced manufacturing campuses run on technicians whose credentials come from community colleges
- Curriculum alignment to specific tenant requirements
- Equipment donation and lab development
- Structured employment pipelines from program completion to Cantus/tenant roles
- Strongest community impact narrative: durable career pathways, median-income employment growth

### Trade Schools and Skilled Trades Programs
- Electricians, instrumentation technicians, mechanical fitters, welders, controls technicians, HVAC specialists
- Multi-hundred-headcount during construction; sustained operational scale during occupancy
- Dual-track programs: classroom + paid apprenticeship at active project sites
- Trainees convert to employees or sub-trade contractors at completion

### Regional Workforce Programs
- State workforce commissions, regional workforce development boards, WIOA operators
- Tap public funding for training that subsidizes tenant-aligned workforce capacity
- Position Cantus as partner of choice when agencies advocate for industrial development incentives

## Internal vs. Tenant Talent

**Cantus operational:** 30-80 personnel per stabilized 500MW campus; multi-hundred construction workforce during build-out.

**Tenant talent offering:** Part of the integrated developer package. Tenants increasingly making site selection decisions based on labor availability over land cost. A developer with a curated talent pipeline has structural advantage in negotiations.

The two profiles reinforce each other — tenant demand justifies pipeline investment that Cantus alone couldn't justify; Cantus demand provides anchor employment that legitimizes partnerships.

## EDC Alignment Strategy

Three levels with differentiated engagement:

### State-Level EDCs
Political and policy partners. Position Cantus as the named developer offered when major recruits ask for power-heavy site capability. Contribute to policy discussions on power infrastructure, workforce, and incentive design.

### Regional EDCs
Deal flow partners. Continuous information exchange on inbound site searches, ongoing negotiations, and infrastructure planning. Visibility into deals 12-24 months ahead of public announcement.

### Local EDCs
Site flow and political alignment partners. Know about unlisted land, receptive landowners, political dynamics, and non-obvious permitting paths. Relationship converts informal information assets into actionable deal flow.

### Deal Flow Mechanics

- **Origination leads** (from local EDCs): 5-15% conversion to closed transactions, compounding with relationship depth
- **Offtake leads** (from state/regional EDCs): 10-25% to active negotiations, 3-8% to closed deals, 12-36 month timelines
- Both categories require 12-24 months of consistent engagement to establish

## Community Impact Framework

| Impact Dimension | Cantus Contribution | Strategic Effect |
|-----------------|--------------------|-----------------| 
| Direct employment | 30-80 operational + multi-hundred construction per campus | Tax base, political support |
| Tenant employment multiplier | 100-1,000+ per major facility; 3-5x construction peak | Major regional employer status |
| Tax base contribution | Property, sales, payroll-driven taxes (post-abatement) | Long-duration municipal revenue |
| Training infrastructure | Equipment donations, programs, lab/curriculum development | Persistent community asset |
| Regional supply chain | Local sourcing of construction, maintenance, food service, security | Small business multiplier |
| Grid and infrastructure | Grid-supportive BTM, ancillary services, peak shaving | Net positive for grid stability |
| Community investment | Scholarships, workforce programs, civic infrastructure | Reciprocal credibility |

The objective: make Cantus the developer that local stakeholders **advocate for** during contested approvals, not the developer they tolerate.

## Geographic Strategy

Depth over breadth. Small set of priority markets with deep investment across all three workstreams, not national coverage. Market selection driven by:

- Power-heavy demand pipeline density
- Infrastructure-dense land availability
- Community receptivity to industrial development at scale

Initial focus: ERCOT and Texas operating relationships. Expansion as intelligence layer (Memo 4) identifies priority targets.

## Sequencing

**Months 0-12:** Initial market selection. First-tier EDC introductions. Scoping university/community college partnerships. Hire community engagement and workforce partnership leadership.

**Months 12-24:** Formal partnership agreements with anchor institutions. First trade school/workforce partnerships operational. EDC engagement cadence established. First campus construction with direct employment outcomes.

**Months 24-36:** Talent pipeline at scale. Tenant talent offering integrated into development packages. EDC alignment generating measurable leads. Community impact demonstrable on first stabilized projects. Adjacent market expansion begins.

**Months 36-60:** Partnership infrastructure replicates across markets. Named partner of choice for major industrial recruitments. Community impact framework structural component of every pitch.
