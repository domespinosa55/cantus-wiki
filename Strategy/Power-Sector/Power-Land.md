# Power Land — Sub-Industry Specification

*Sub-industry 4 of 10 in the Power sector framework. Drafting. This is the sub-industry in which Cantus operates as a direct principal — Cantus Development.*

---

## 1. Definition

Power Land is the sub-industry of **real estate assembled, entitled, and configured specifically for power-intensive industrial use**. The unit of production is the site — assembled acreage carrying the specific combination of interconnection rights, transmission proximity, large-load tariff eligibility, water access, fiber access, zoning entitlements, road and rail access, and labor-shed access that makes it suitable for a Power Industries operator at scale.

The boundary against adjacent Power sub-industries:

- **Power Assets** owns the generation, transmission, substation, and storage infrastructure sited on the land. Power Land does not own the kit on top of the dirt; it owns the dirt and the bundle of rights attached to it.
- **Power Industries** operates the industrial process inside the fence. Power Land provides the substrate the operator builds on.
- **Power Services** prepares, builds, and maintains. Land entitles, assembles, and configures.

The boundary against **traditional industrial real estate** is what defines the sub-industry. Standard industrial real estate (logistics warehouses, light manufacturing parks) optimizes for highway access, truck-court depth, dock-door ratios, and labor shed. Power Land overlays power-specific conditions: explicit interconnection rights (queued, studied, or executed), transmission capacity adjacency, large-load tariff eligibility, water rights at scale (now an industrial-scale concern given AI cooling demand), and zoning that contemplates 24/7 industrial use at hundreds of megawatts. These overlays are not edge features; they are the primary value drivers. The same acre with and without them differs in market value by 50–100x (cross-reference [[_parameters]]: $2,500–$10,000/acre raw vs. ~$800K/acre powered in Texas).

Within Power Land, eight structural sub-segments:

1. **Greenfield assembled tracts** — raw land aggregated and entitled for power-intensive industrial use.
2. **Powered industrial sites** — single-tenant or small multi-tenant pads with confirmed interconnection rights and entitlements.
3. **Multi-tenant power campuses** — the Cantus Development product class: integrated multi-tenant developments with shared infrastructure.
4. **Data center parks** — typically embedded inside MPC developer portfolios (AllianceTexas, others).
5. **Energy parks** — sites where utility-scale generation is the anchor and industrial demand layers on.
6. **Brownfield repositioned sites** — former industrial sites (steel mills, coal plants, refineries) with existing power and interconnection that are repositioned for new power-intensive use.
7. **Mineral and energy rights bundles** — particularly relevant in Texas, where surface, mineral, water, and groundwater rights are separable and tradeable.
8. **Special-purpose sites** — hydrogen hubs, advanced manufacturing zones, e-fuel production sites, sites with specialty entitlements (export licensing, port adjacency, defense-related access).

## 2. Deliverables

Power Land produces five classes of deliverable, increasingly layered:

**Raw land control** — deeded ownership, option contracts, ground lease, right-of-first-refusal. Site control is the first product. Without it, none of the layers above can be built.

**Entitled land** — zoning approved for power-intensive industrial use, environmental clearance (Phase I, Phase II if needed, USACE 404 where wetlands intersect), state environmental clearance (TCEQ air, water in Texas), tribal consultation cleared, endangered species review cleared, water rights confirmed, road and rail access secured.

**Interconnection-attached land** — the differentiating layer. Sites with queued, studied, or executed interconnection positions trade at a structural premium over otherwise equivalent acreage. The interconnection right is functionally part of the land bundle.

**Configured site** — graded, prepared, with utilities to fence, civil work complete, ready for vertical construction. This is the deliverable that an EPC contractor or tenant takes possession of.

**Master-planned campus** — multi-site, integrated-infrastructure, multi-tenant development. The Cantus Intelligent Campus is this layer. Combines configured pads, shared substation, shared water, shared fiber, shared workforce-and-community function.

The contractual instruments are real estate instruments: warranty deeds, ground leases, build-to-suit leases, joint development agreements, option agreements, easements. The combination with interconnection rights and tariff eligibility is what makes the bundle a Power Land product rather than a real estate product.

### Permian / West Texas land

The overlap of surface, mineral, water, and groundwater rights in West Texas creates multi-use value that pure-Power-Land elsewhere cannot replicate.

### Data center real estate (cross-boundary with Power Industries operator class)

Digital Realty (DLR; owns underlying real estate); Equinix (EQIX); CyrusOne (KKR/GIP-owned); Aligned Data Centers; Compass Datacenters; Stack Infrastructure; Switch (DigitalBridge-owned); DataBank; Cologix; QTS Realty (Blackstone-owned); T5 Data Centers; Vantage Data Centers; NTT GDC; Stream Data Centers. These firms operate at the boundary — the underlying real estate is Power Land; the operating data hall is Power Industries. The framework convention is that the firm's primary commercial product (colocation services, lease revenue) classifies it as Power Industries; the real estate it sits on is Power Land owned by the same entity.

### Hyperscale self-developers (direct land assembly)

Microsoft, AWS, Google, Meta, Oracle each operate direct land acquisition teams. These teams aggregate sites at scale, often through brokerage networks and JVs with MPC developers. They are simultaneously a counterparty class and a competitor class for Power Land.

### Renewable generation land aggregators

Most utility-scale generation projects assemble land directly through the IPP (NextEra, Invenergy, Pattern, Pine Gate, Clearway). Specialized land aggregators serve the renewable build-out — First Solar's land bank, Cypress Creek's land team, and regional brokerages with renewable-specific practices.

### Public-sector and quasi-public

Texas General Land Office (Permanent School Fund); state land offices in other states (Oklahoma, New Mexico, North Dakota, Wyoming); BLM (federal land for energy projects); USFS; tribal nations (sovereign control); Texas Pacific Land Trust (TPL — now TPL Corporation, the Permian land trust).

## 4. Capital Structures and Economics

Power Land is real estate, so it follows real estate capital structures. The specific economics for power-intensive use differ from logistics or office real estate in three respects: the capital structure tolerates long-duration land banking, the value creation is rights-driven rather than improvement-driven, and the underwrite depends on power-system parameters (interconnection, transmission, ISO/RTO dynamics) that real estate underwriting does not natively model.

### Capital intensity by stage

| Stage | $/acre, Texas reference |
|---|---|
| Raw rural / agricultural | 2,500–10,000 |
| Entitled industrial (without confirmed power) | 25,000–75,000 |
| Powered, entitled industrial pad | ~800,000 |
| Configured campus pad (graded, utilities to fence) | 1,000,000–1,500,000 |
| Operating data center pad (rare to trade) | 2,000,000+ |

Cross-reference [[_parameters]] Mixed-Tenant Campus Economics. The ~100x premium from raw to powered is the structural arbitrage that Cantus Development monetizes. The arbitrage exists because the entitlement and interconnection rights are not commodities; they are positions in queues, agreements with utilities, and political relationships with regulators that cannot be procured on demand.

### Capital structures by holder type

| Capital type | Typical hold | Target return |
|---|---|---|
| Land banking PE / family office | 10–25 yr | 12–18% IRR |
| Sovereign wealth (very long duration) | 20+ yr | 8–12% IRR |
| MPC developer balance sheet | 5–15 yr | 15–25% IRR (developer profit) |
| REIT (entitled industrial) | Permanent | 5–7% cap rate (entitled pad), lower for built |
| Joint venture (cash + carry) | 5–10 yr | 15–25% IRR to carry |
| Dedicated infrastructure fund (powered land overlay) | 7–15 yr | 12–17% IRR |

Cantus Development's Fund I architecture per [[Strategy/Primary-Inputs-01-Land-Aggregation]] sits in the dedicated-infrastructure-fund row, with the Cantus-specific overlay (interconnection-attached land plus integrated demand orchestration).

### Texas-specific dynamics

- No state income tax
- Chapter 313 replaced by Chapter 403 (HB 5, 2023) — modified property-tax abatement framework for industrial projects
- Limited zoning in unincorporated areas — entitlement is materially easier than coastal or Northeast markets
- Strong private property rights and active landowner economy (oil & gas heritage means many landowners are sophisticated counterparties)
- ERCOT load growth concentrates demand for sites in specific transmission corridors
- Surface, mineral, water, and groundwater rights are separable and tradeable — creates layered economics where one tract can carry multiple revenue streams

### Brownfield economics

Brownfield repositioning (former steel mills, coal plants, refineries, paper mills) is a meaningful sub-segment because **existing interconnection rights survive site repurposing**. A 500MW coal plant retiring keeps its 500MW interconnection position; converting the site to a hyperscale campus or BTM gas plus battery monetizes that position without the multi-year queue wait. Environmental liability (remediation, asbestos, soil contamination) is the offset.

## 6. Regulatory Environment

Power Land regulation operates primarily at the **state and local** level (entitlement, zoning, water, mineral rights), with a federal overlay for environmental review and specific easement classes.

**Federal**

- NEPA — environmental review for federal-nexus projects, federal land siting, federal financing
- USACE — Section 404 wetlands permitting
- USFWS — endangered species (Section 7 consultation for federal nexus; Section 10 for incidental take)
- BLM and USFS — federal land siting
- FAA — height restrictions and aviation impact (relevant for wind, certain tall structures)
- Tribal consultation — Section 106 (cultural resources), other federal frameworks

**State (Texas as the canonical example for Cantus)**

- TCEQ — air, water, waste permitting at state level
- Railroad Commission of Texas — oil & gas surface use, pipelines, intrastate gas pipelines
- Texas Water Development Board, groundwater conservation districts — water rights administration (groundwater under rule of capture with conservation district overlay; surface water under prior appropriation)
- Texas PUC — interconnection rights, transmission corridors
- Texas Comptroller — property tax administration, abatement program (HB 5 / Chapter 403)
- TxDOT — road access permits
- Texas Department of Agriculture — pesticide applicator zones, in rural land use

**Local**

- County or municipal zoning — limited in unincorporated Texas areas, more restrictive in incorporated areas
- ETJ (extraterritorial jurisdiction) — Texas-specific city-county overlap zone
- School districts — property tax, water and road coordination
- MUD (municipal utility district) and PID (public improvement district) creation — special-purpose tax and utility districts
- Local water authorities

**Mineral, water, and groundwater**

Texas treats these separately from surface estate. A landowner may sell surface and retain minerals (typical for Permian and other oil-and-gas-active regions); groundwater is technically a separate estate as well. Power Land underwriting in Texas requires clean title analysis across all three estates and surface use agreements where they are severed.

**Easements**

Oil and gas pipelines, telecommunications, transmission, road easements, conservation easements. The aggregate easement footprint on a candidate Power Land tract is often the deciding factor on usability for high-intensity industrial development.

**Eminent domain**

In Texas, TSPs and common carriers (including some renewable transmission developers) hold eminent domain authority for transmission. Industrial users do not. This shapes corridor strategy and the negotiating posture with landowners along transmission paths.

## 7. Cantus's Relationship to Power Land

This is one of two sub-industries in which Cantus operates as a **direct principal** (the other being [[Strategy/Power-Sector/Power-Facilities]]). Under the v2 four-vertical architecture, both Power Land and Power Facilities are contained within the **Cantus Development** vertical, which is the firm's site-creation engine: assemble Land + build Facility = create the productive site. The Development vertical's role in the firm's value-creation sequence is "Development creates the sites."

### By role

- **Greenfield land assembler.** Cantus Development aggregates raw acreage in target corridors (the ERCOT pipeline near-term and large sites within 50 miles of Houston CBD per [[_parameters]]).
- **Entitler.** Zoning, environmental, water rights, transmission access, interconnection-queue position. The entitlement work is the value creation; the dollar arbitrage from raw to entitled to powered is the primary Cantus Development economics.
- **Site owner / ground lessor / build-to-suit landlord.** Cantus Development holds title or long-tenor leasehold; tenants take ground lease or build-to-suit positions.
- **Master-planner.** Multi-tenant Cantus Intelligent Campuses with shared substation, shared water, shared fiber, shared workforce infrastructure.
- **Counterparty to MPC developer partners (Hillwood, Crow, Howard Hughes, Majestic).** Some Cantus Development sites will be assembled independently; others through JV with Tier-1 MPC developers per [[_parameters]] Tier 1 Co-Development Partners. The JV model lets Cantus Development combine the powered-industrial intelligence with the MPC developer's master-planning, capital, and political relationships.
- **Brownfield repositioner.** Cantus Development is positioned to acquire retiring industrial sites (coal plants, steel, refineries) and reposition their interconnection rights. This is the only Power Land sub-segment where Cantus's information advantage (knowing which sites have transferable interconnection rights and what their tariff treatment looks like) is decisive.
- **Site brand owner.** Over time, Cantus campus brand (as the Intelligent Campus) becomes a marketing and capital asset.

### By phase

**Building (Years 1–2) — current.** Principal land control on first sites; JVs with Tier-1 partners as deal architecture allows; building intelligence on Texas land market dynamics. CII Parcel Registry of 50+ scored sites in ERCOT per [[_parameters]] is the underlying intelligence asset.

**Activation (Years 3–4) — modeled.** Cantus-led campuses with principal positions; Fund I capital ($250M–$500M per [[_parameters]]) deployed to land assembly and entitlement.

**Compounding (Years 5–7) — modeled.** Multi-campus portfolio, fund-owned land bank, brand recognition. Brownfield repositioning at scale.

**Mature platform (Years 8+).** Platform land bank at a scale comparable to Hillwood or Crow's powered-industrial portfolio, with the Cantus differentiator being the integrated demand-side intelligence overlay.

### What Cantus is not (in Power Land)

Not a residential MPC developer. Not a logistics REIT. Not a pure data center REIT. Not a renewable land aggregator standalone. Cantus Development's specific posture is **multi-tenant powered industrial assembly with integrated demand-side intelligence**, which is structurally adjacent to but distinct from each of the above.

### The principal claim

The structural claim Cantus makes about Power Land is that the sub-industry is not yet recognized as its own asset class. Powered-industrial real estate has been treated as a sub-segment of industrial real estate (Prologis universe), of MPC development (Hillwood/Crow/Howard Hughes universe), or of data center real estate (Digital Realty/Equinix universe). None of these categorizations captures the explicit power-overlay value creation. Cantus Development's thesis is that powered-industrial real estate is its own asset class, with its own underwriting parameters, its own capital pool, and its own returns profile. The firm constructs the category and the platform simultaneously.

## 8. Key Trends and Structural Conditions (2026–2036)

**1. Powered land premium expansion.** The ~100x premium from raw to powered per [[_parameters]] is widening as interconnection rights become scarcer in ERCOT, PJM, MISO. The structural driver is the interconnection queue collapse documented in [[Strategy/Power-Sector/Power-Assets]]. Sites with executed interconnection are pricing at multiples of sites with only queued interconnection, which themselves price above unrelated industrial acreage.

**2. Texas land assembly is the most acute US market.** Population growth + ERCOT load growth + industrial reshoring + state policy alignment + no income tax + limited zoning makes Texas the single most active Power Land market. The next-most-active are Virginia (Loudoun County, Mecklenburg County), Arizona (Phoenix metropolitan), Georgia (greater Atlanta), and select Midwest manufacturing corridors. Cantus's Texas concentration is consistent with where the market is.

**3. Permian Basin multi-use overlap.** Land control in West Texas where O&G surface use, power infrastructure, and water rights overlap is creating layered economics that pure-play land elsewhere cannot replicate. TPL Corporation, LandBridge, and the larger Permian family holdings are the comparable platforms. Cantus has not yet articulated a Permian-specific Land strategy; this is a candidate for explicit treatment in the business plan rewrite.

**4. Water rights as binding industrial input.** AI cooling demand at hyperscale is making water rights structurally relevant. Texas groundwater conservation districts and surface water rights are increasingly limiting. Some sites that pencil on power do not pencil on water; the framework needs to address water-rights underwriting explicitly.

**5. MPC developer recalibration.** Hillwood, Crow, Howard Hughes, and Majestic have all built dedicated powered-industrial platforms over 2023–2025. The Tier-1 partner cohort per [[_parameters]] is increasingly competitive with rather than complementary to Cantus on greenfield assembly; the JV architecture has to navigate this tension. Differentiation is moving from "can you assemble" to "can you integrate."

**6. State industrial development incentive framework.** HB 5 in Texas (Chapter 403, the Chapter 313 replacement), Opportunity Zone extensions, state-specific battery and semiconductor incentives, and federal CHIPS-and-IRA-driven state coordination all affect site economics. Underwriting Power Land requires modeling incentive packages by site; the static-cost analysis underprices a site by 15–40% in some markets.

**7. Interconnection-attached land valuation.** Sites with executed or queue-advanced interconnection positions are pricing at structural premium. This has created speculative interconnection-queue positions held purely for resale value; ERCOT and PJM are reforming queue rules to limit speculation. Cantus's CII Parcel Registry tracks interconnection-attached pricing as a primary data asset.

**8. Environmental review timeline lengthening.** Wetlands (USACE 404), endangered species (USFWS Section 7), tribal consultation, and NEPA review are all running longer in 2024–2026 than historical norms. The federal-nexus avoidance strategy (private land, non-federal financing) materially shortens entitlement timeline. This is a primary input to Texas's advantage.

**9. Brownfield repositioning at scale.** Coal plant retirements, steel mill closures, and refinery rationalizations are creating sites with transferable interconnection rights. The brownfield-to-AI-campus playbook (most visibly Microsoft–Constellation TMI, but replicated at smaller scale across multiple coal sites) is becoming standard. Cantus Development's information advantage on which brownfields carry transferable rights is decisive.

**10. Hyperscaler self-development at scale.** Microsoft, AWS, Google, Meta, and Oracle are direct land assemblers at scale, often through brokerage networks and JVs. They are simultaneously the primary customer class for Cantus Development and the primary competitor for the best sites. The structural question is whether the hyperscaler's vertical integration extends to the integrated multi-tenant campus product, or whether the multi-tenant integration is something they continue to outsource. Cantus's thesis assumes the latter for most use cases.

## 9. Open Questions for the Framework Rewrite

To resolve as the framework matures:

- **Should Cantus Development develop an explicit Permian strategy?** The multi-use overlap (surface, mineral, water, power) creates economics not available elsewhere. TPL and LandBridge are the platform comparables. The framework should articulate whether Cantus operates in the Permian, partners into it, or stays out.
- **How does the framework treat water rights as a constituent of Power Land or as a separate Power-sector function?** Water is currently nested inside Power Land. Given the scale of AI cooling demand, water rights may merit elevation to a parallel concern. To resolve before the Layer-1 rewrite.
- **JV architecture with Tier-1 MPC developers.** As the Tier-1 cohort builds competing powered-industrial platforms, the JV terms (carry, control, brand) need explicit calibration. The framework should specify the Cantus-led vs. partner-led split criteria.
- **Brand strategy for Cantus Intelligent Campus.** The campus brand is a long-tenor capital asset. The framework should address whether the brand is master-branded (every campus is a Cantus Intelligent Campus) or sub-branded (each campus has its own brand under a Cantus parent).
- **Interconnection rights as a separable Power Land asset class.** Should "interconnection rights" be treated as a tradeable position with its own underwriting, separate from the land underneath? Some market participants are starting to do this. The framework should take a position.
