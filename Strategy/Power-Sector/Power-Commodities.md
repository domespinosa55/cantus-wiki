# Power Commodities — Sub-Industry Specification

*Sub-industry 6 of 10 in the Power sector framework. Drafting. Cantus Markets advises and structures within this sub-industry without producing or trading commodities.*

---

## 1. Definition

Power Commodities is the sub-industry of **high-value commodities produced by Power Industries operators**, plus the market infrastructure that finds price, transfers risk, and clears trades on those commodities. The unit of production is the *output unit* of a Power Industries process — a unit of compute capacity, a token, a battery cell, a semiconductor wafer, a ton of refined critical mineral, a kilogram of clean hydrogen, a vial of biological product, a defense article, a unit of robot labor capacity — together with the financial and informational layer that turns those output units into a market. The v2 framework adds "robot labor capacity" to the canonical commodity list, anticipating the commercialization of robotics-as-a-service output as humanoid and industrial robotics commercialization scales over 2026–2030.

The defining condition is that the commodity exists *because* of large-scale power consumption upstream, and its market dynamics reflect that upstream condition. Standard physical commodities (corn, crude oil, copper ore) are not Power Commodities; their production is not power-driven at the dominant scale. Refined lithium hydroxide is a Power Commodity because the refining is fundamentally power-intensive and the price is bid by buyers whose ability to use it is itself power-dependent. The framework treats Power Commodities as a distinct asset class precisely because the upstream Power-sector dynamics propagate into the commodity's pricing, the contracts, and the financialization layer.

The boundary against adjacent Power sub-industries:

- **Power Industries** operates the production. Power Commodities is the *output* of that production and the market through which the output trades. The same firm produces both, but the operating plant and the traded output are different deliverables with different counterparties.
- **Power Equipment** is capital goods sold to Power Assets. Power Commodities is consumable or financializable output sold downstream. A turbine is Equipment; a megawatt-hour produced by the turbine and sold into a market is closer to a Power Commodity (treated in Power Markets); a refined cobalt ingot is a Power Commodity.
- **Power Markets** trades wholesale electricity and ancillary services — electrons themselves as a financial instrument. Power Commodities is the downstream commodity layer (cells, wafers, hydrogen, refined metals) that exists *because* Power Markets has cleared the upstream electrons. The two sub-industries share an analytical method (commodity-market structure) but operate on different commodities.

Within Power Commodities, two structural classes:

**A. Physical Power Commodities** — produced units that trade as physical goods:

1. **Compute commodity** — compute-hours, GPU-hours, inference tokens, training-hours. Emerging as a tradeable commodity; standardization is in early stages.
2. **Battery commodity** — lithium cells, modules, packs, recycled cathode material when traded as commodities into the cell market.
3. **Semiconductor commodity** — wafers, dies, advanced packaging substrates, displays.
4. **Critical minerals commodity** — refined lithium hydroxide and carbonate, nickel sulfate, cobalt sulfate, refined rare-earth oxides and metals, graphite anode material, gallium, germanium, antimony.
5. **Hydrogen commodity** — green, blue, pink (nuclear) hydrogen with certificate-of-origin attribution.
6. **E-fuels and carbon-removed fuels** — sustainable aviation fuel (SAF), methanol, ammonia, e-diesel.
7. **Biological commodities** — biopharma ingredients and finished biologics, bioindustrial products, synthetic-biology-derived chemicals.
8. **Defense commodities** — munitions, drones, missile bodies, energetic materials, large structures (where the DoD procurement framework treats them as quasi-commodities under multi-year contracting).

**B. Financialized Power Commodity instruments** — financial and informational layers built on the underlying:

1. **Green attribute certificates** — RECs, GOs, EACs, hydrogen certificates of origin.
2. **Carbon credits** — voluntary and compliance-market credits, particularly those generated by Power Industries processes.
3. **Compute capacity futures and structured products** — emerging.
4. **Critical mineral and battery futures** — LME and CME instruments, OTC structured products.
5. **Insurance-linked products** — price-risk insurance on commodity output for tenants and producers.

## 2. Deliverables

Power Commodities produces four classes of deliverable, layered:

**The physical commodity unit** — the unit of output (cell, wafer, kg of H₂, ton of refined Co, vial, GPU-hour). Specified by purity, grade, performance, attribution (where origin matters), delivery, and quality.

**The financial instrument** — the contract, certificate, or future that monetizes the underlying physical commodity. Bilateral offtake contracts, futures contracts, options, swaps, certificates of origin.

**Price discovery and market structure** — the index, exchange, or assessment that establishes price. BNEF battery price survey, Benchmark Mineral Intelligence price assessment, Fastmarkets rare-earth assessment, Argus Hydrogen Index, Platts Clean Hydrogen Assessment, LME refined-metal contracts, CME lithium hydroxide futures, voluntary carbon market indices.

**Information products** — research, intelligence, and analytics that make the market legible. This is structurally important because most Power Commodity markets are still in price-discovery phase, and the information layer is one of the assets being built.

The contractual instruments are familiar from any commodity sub-industry: bilateral offtake agreements (5–15 year for large-scale industrial output), spot purchase agreements, futures and options, swap contracts (fixed-for-floating), structured offtake products (capacity-linked, performance-linked).

## 3. Major Players (US-Focused)

### Compute commodity markets

AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle Cloud (selling compute capacity as a commodity to enterprise); CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, RunPod (AI compute as a more commodity-like product); Vast.ai (decentralized GPU marketplace); Bittensor (decentralized inference markets); io.net (Solana-based GPU); Akash Network (Cosmos-based). The compute commodity is the most actively forming in this list — the absence of a true commodity exchange for compute, the heterogeneity of GPU types, and the API and SLA fragmentation are the binding problems.

### Battery cell markets

Major cell producers (CATL, BYD, LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, SK On, Panasonic) are the producers; cell pricing transparency comes from BNEF Lithium-Ion Battery Price Survey, Benchmark Mineral Intelligence cell price assessments, Fastmarkets cell prices. Spot market for surplus cells exists but is opaque; most cell trade is contracted. CME lithium hydroxide and cobalt futures provide partial hedging proxies.

### Semiconductor commodity markets

TSMC, Samsung Foundry, Intel, GlobalFoundries as the foundry-as-commodity-seller. Spot market for older nodes (28nm and above) is meaningful; advanced nodes (5nm and below) are fully contracted. DigiTimes, IDC, Gartner, TrendForce as market data providers. SEMI as the industry standards body. Wafer Industry Database for pricing reference.

### Solar PV module markets

BNEF Solar Module Price Survey, PV InfoLink, OPIS (Energy Acuity acquired) for module price tracking. Spot market for Chinese-origin modules; contracted market for US-made (First Solar, Qcells, etc.). Solar Tradex futures contracts exist but with limited liquidity.

### Hydrogen markets

Air Products, Linde, Plug Power as merchant sellers. Argus Hydrogen Price Index, S&P Platts Hydrogen Assessment, ICIS Hydrogen for price discovery. Bilateral contracted offtake is the dominant market structure; spot market is thin. Certificate-of-origin systems (EU CertifHy, US RIN-adjacent emerging) are developing.

### Refined critical minerals

LME contracts for lithium hydroxide, cobalt, nickel; Fastmarkets and Benchmark Mineral Intelligence for rare earths; Shanghai Metals Market for Chinese price discovery; SMM, Argus, S&P Global Market Intelligence as additional providers. Physical trade is heavily bilateral; financialization is increasing.

### REC / Green attribute markets

M-RETS (Midwest), ERCOT REC, WREGIS (Western), PJM-GATS (PJM), NEPOOL-GIS (New England); voluntary Green-e Certified; international I-REC.

### Carbon markets

Verra, Gold Standard, Climate Action Reserve, ACR (American Carbon Registry) for voluntary; California cap-and-trade, RGGI (Northeast), Washington CCA for compliance; CME nature-based futures; ICE compliance carbon futures.

### Defense commodity (DoD as buyer)

Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, General Dynamics, Boeing Defense, BAE Systems Inc., Anduril, Shield AI as producers; DoD as the single dominant buyer; multi-year procurement contracts under DFARS as the commodity-like structure.

### Major commodity trading firms (potentially active in Power Commodities)

Trafigura (metals, energy); Glencore (metals, energy); Vitol (energy); Mercuria (energy); Cargill (specialty); Castleton Commodities (energy); Hartree Partners; ConocoPhillips (gas trading); Shell Trading; BP Trading. The major commodity trading houses have expanded into Power Commodities — particularly battery materials, hydrogen, and carbon — over 2023–2026.

### Hyperscaler-as-trader

Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta have growing commodity-trading capabilities for power, RECs, carbon, hydrogen attribution, and increasingly for the upstream materials that feed their fabrication chains. These desks are not pure-play trading firms but they are meaningful market participants.

### OEM-as-trader

Battery OEMs (LG, Samsung, CATL, BYD) trade lithium and cobalt to hedge cell margin; auto OEMs (Tesla, Ford, GM, Volkswagen) trade and contract upstream cathode materials. The OEM trading capability is increasingly material to upstream commodity price formation.

## 4. Capital Structures and Economics

Power Commodities is not a capital-intensive sub-industry in the way Power Assets or Power Industries are. It is a **market-infrastructure** sub-industry — capital is deployed in trading positions, inventory financing, structured products, clearing, and market data. The economics resemble commodity trading and asset management more than industrial production.

### Capital deployed by participant class

| Participant class | Capital deployed | Returns target |
|---|---|---|
| Commodity trading house (Trafigura, Glencore class) | Trading capital + inventory finance | 15–25% on trading capital |
| Producer trading desk (LG, CATL, Tesla, hyperscaler) | Internal capital for hedging + opportunistic | Hedge effectiveness measured, not P&L return |
| Structured products provider (banks, specialists) | Risk capital | 12–20% RoE |
| Asset manager (commodity-linked funds, ETFs) | Investor capital | 8–14% net return target |
| Sovereign / strategic stockpile | Government capital | Strategic, not return-driven |
| Exchange / clearing house (LME, CME, ICE) | Operating capital | Steady transaction-fee economics |
| Market data and assessment firm (BNEF, Benchmark, Fastmarkets, Platts) | Operating capital | High-margin subscription business |
| Insurance / risk transfer | Underwriting capital | Combined ratio < 1.0 |

### Margins by activity class

| Activity | Margin reference |
|---|---|
| Physical commodity trading (Trafigura, Glencore comp) | 1–5% net margin on revenue; high turnover |
| Structured products | 5–15% on notional, varying with risk |
| Market data subscription | 60–80% gross margin |
| Exchange operations | 50–70% operating margin (mature) |
| Insurance underwriting | Variable; depends on loss ratio |

### Market liquidity continuum

Power Commodities markets sit at different points on the liquidity spectrum:

| Commodity | Liquidity stage |
|---|---|
| Wholesale electricity (Power Markets) | Mature, highly liquid in deregulated US markets |
| RECs | Mature, regional |
| Verra / Gold Standard voluntary carbon | Mature but quality-disputed |
| LME refined metals (Ni, Co) | Mature |
| LME / CME lithium | Developing |
| Battery cell spot | Opaque, bilateral-dominated |
| Solar modules (Chinese-origin) | Liquid, opaque, bilaterally cleared |
| Solar modules (US-made) | Contracted; spot market thin |
| Hydrogen | Pre-commercial liquidity; bilateral offtake dominant |
| E-fuels / SAF | Pre-commercial liquidity |
| Compute capacity | Forming; no true commodity exchange yet |
| Semiconductor wafers (advanced node) | Contracted-only; no spot |
| Semiconductor wafers (mature node) | Modest spot market |
| Defense articles | Government-procured, not market-cleared |
| Biopharma intermediates | Contracted dominant |
| Refined rare earths | Assessment-driven, Chinese-influenced |
| Critical minerals stockpile | Strategic accumulation rather than market |

### Information economics

The market-data and assessment layer has structurally high-margin economics. BNEF (Bloomberg subsidiary), S&P Global Market Intelligence, Wood Mackenzie, Benchmark Mineral Intelligence, Fastmarkets, Argus, and ICIS operate as the information infrastructure for Power Commodities. The economic structure resembles a financial market data subscription business with embedded research and consulting. New entrants are forming in compute (Cloud Price Index attempts), hydrogen (multiple), and defense procurement intelligence.

## 6. Regulatory Environment

Power Commodities is the **most fragmented** Power-sector sub-industry from a regulatory standpoint, because each commodity class is regulated under its own framework rather than under a unifying Power-sector regulator.

**Federal financial regulators**

- **CFTC** — futures and options markets; jurisdiction over commodity derivative trading; oversight of LME and CME-listed contracts touching US persons.
- **SEC** — commodity-linked securities, ETFs, structured notes; spot-market commodity oversight where it intersects with securities.
- **FERC** — wholesale power markets (more relevant to Power Markets, but tangential here when REC or capacity transactions touch the commodity).
- **OFAC** — sanctioned-party exposure; specific countries and entities.
- **BIS** — export controls (especially semiconductors, biotech, defense, critical minerals processing tech).

**Trade**

- **DOC and USTR** — AD/CVD on specific commodities (lithium, magnets, solar modules, batteries); Section 232 national security tariffs; Section 301 China tariffs.
- **CBP** — customs enforcement.

**Sectoral commodity oversight**

- **EPA** — environmental commodities (RECs, RINs); also industrial-process emissions that create or compete with carbon commodities.
- **DOE** — strategic stockpiles, critical materials office, hydrogen attribution.
- **DoD** — defense commodity procurement; National Defense Stockpile; DPA Title III commodity programs.
- **FDA and DEA** — biological commodities; controlled substances and precursors.
- **NRC** — nuclear-relevant commodities (uranium, certain isotopes).
- **State PUCs and state agencies** — REC programs, state carbon programs (California CCA, RGGI, Washington CCA).

**Voluntary market governance**

- **Verra (VCS), Gold Standard, Climate Action Reserve, ACR** — voluntary carbon market registries and methodology approval; subject to ICVCM Core Carbon Principles.
- **Green-e Certified** — voluntary REC program.
- **I-REC, EAC** — international green attribute systems.

**Sanctions and export-control regime**

The Russia-Ukraine war, China-Taiwan tensions, and broader great-power competition have made sanctions and export-control regulation a structurally larger factor in Power Commodities than in any prior cycle. Critical-mineral export controls, semiconductor manufacturing equipment export controls, and battery FEOC rules are the most acute current examples; the broader pattern is expanding.

## 7. Cantus's Relationship to Power Commodities

Cantus does not produce Power Commodities. Power Commodities sits inside the **Cantus Markets** vertical in the v2 four-vertical architecture (alongside Power Industries and Power Markets-broad). Cantus Markets **advises and structures** within Power Commodities without operating production or running a trading desk. The vertical provides analytical, structuring, and capital-routing services around Power Commodities for Cantus campus tenants, Cantus capital partners, and selected third-party engagements. "Markets monetizes and finances the output" in the firm's value-creation sequence.

### By role

- **Offtake structurer.** When a Cantus Industries customer needs to monetize output — a battery factory selling cells, a hydrogen producer selling H₂, a fab selling wafers, an AI compute operator selling capacity — Cantus Markets can structure the offtake agreement, route to creditworthy counterparties, and embed hedge structures.
- **Commodity hedging advisor.** When a Cantus campus tenant has commodity exposure (cell price, lithium, REC, carbon, hydrogen), Cantus Markets advises on hedging structure without acting as the hedge counterparty.
- **Capital partner matchmaker for commodity-linked structured products.** Cantus's capital partners include sovereign wealth and institutional infrastructure investors with appetite for commodity-linked structured products; matching these capital pools to Cantus tenant commodity exposure is a structurable activity.
- **Intelligence source.** Power Commodity market data and intelligence is one of the CII data assets; see [[Products/CII-Iteration-Document]]. The Cantus relationship with tenants and counterparties feeds market intelligence that informs both internal underwriting and external advisory.
- **Compute commodity advocacy.** The compute commodity is the emerging frontier most directly tied to Cantus's Compute-vector tenant base. Cantus is positioned to participate in the formation of compute as a tradeable commodity — through standards, through structured offtake, through tenant-side aggregation — without becoming a trading desk.
- **Not a producer. Not a trading desk. Not a market maker. Not a hedging counterparty in principal.**

### By phase

**Building (Years 1–2) — current.** Light advisory engagements with tenants on commodity exposure; intelligence-gathering on Power Commodity markets; building Cantus's intellectual position on Power Commodities as an emerging asset class.

**Activation (Years 3–4) — modeled.** Structured offtake advisory on Cantus-led campus deals; capital partner matchmaking for commodity-linked structured products; possibly initiating a compute-commodity working group with tenant partners.

**Compounding (Years 5–7) — modeled.** Possibly a small structured-products advisory desk serving Cantus campus customers and capital partners. Possibly market-making activity in a single, high-clarity Power Commodity (most likely compute capacity or a campus-specific tenant-grouped product).

**Mature platform (Years 8+).** Cantus Markets could host structured commodity products tied to Cantus campus output — a recognized intermediary in compute-commodity structuring, with continued advisory across the other vectors.

### The structuring claim

The structural claim Cantus makes about Power Commodities is that the sub-industry is in **price-discovery and market-formation phase**, particularly for compute, hydrogen, and battery commodities. The participants who structure the market formation — set standards, build indices, originate the first generation of contracts — capture asymmetric returns relative to spot-traders. Cantus is positioned to participate in market formation through its tenant relationships, capital partner network, and intelligence layer, without committing to trading-desk capital exposure. The integrator role at the demand-side level translates naturally into the structuring role at the commodity level.

## 8. Key Trends and Structural Conditions (2026–2036)

**1. Compute commodity formation.** The dominant Power Commodity story of the next decade. Compute is not yet a true commodity — heterogeneous GPU types, API and SLA fragmentation, billing units that vary by provider — but the structural drivers of commoditization (large pools of capacity, fungible workloads on standardized accelerators, growing third-party operators, vendor financing that wants to amortize) are accelerating. The first cohort of structured compute offtake contracts, futures, and capacity options is in early formation. The party that defines the standard captures structural value.

**2. Battery cell pricing transparency.** BNEF, Benchmark, Fastmarkets cell pricing has become daily benchmark in supply contracts. CME and LME lithium derivatives are growing. Cell pricing is increasingly a commodity-market function rather than a bilateral OEM-OEM exchange.

**3. Hydrogen index formation.** Argus, Platts, ICIS are all building hydrogen indices. The 45V structure (and any successor) embeds commodity attribution (green, blue, pink, gray) into the credit math, which forces commodity-grade attribution and creates the substrate for an indexed market. Liquidity is years away but the architecture is being built now.

**4. Critical minerals financialization.** LME lithium hydroxide, CME refined metals expansion, OTC structured products for rare earths and graphite. The combination of US/allied reshoring and Chinese pricing dominance is creating the conditions for non-Chinese price discovery; the early platforms are forming.

**5. REC and carbon market evolution under quality scrutiny.** Voluntary carbon market quality concerns (Verra credit invalidation events, Gold Standard methodology revisions) have repriced the market. ICVCM Core Carbon Principles, IC-VCM ratings, and increasing institutional demand for high-quality credits are driving a quality-segmented market. RECs less affected but tracking quality-segmentation pressure as well.

**6. Defense commodity formation.** The DoD push for multi-year procurement under DPA authority is creating quasi-commodity contracting for munitions, drones, missile bodies, and energetic materials. Anduril, Shield AI, and the new defense-tech cohort are scaling alongside the primes in ways that look more like commodity supply than bespoke contracting. This is a structurally new pattern.

**7. Power Commodity index funds and structured products at the retail layer.** Battery-supply ETFs, critical-minerals ETFs, compute-infrastructure-themed ETFs are emerging. The retail capital pool is being introduced to Power Commodities as a thematic exposure.

**8. Trading house consolidation and capability expansion.** Trafigura, Glencore, Vitol, Mercuria are expanding from energy/metals into battery materials, hydrogen, carbon, and compute-adjacent activities. The trading houses' integrated logistics, financing, and counterparty-network capabilities translate into Power Commodities in ways that pure-financial banks struggle to replicate.

**9. Sovereign commodity stockpiling.** US National Defense Stockpile expansion (post-FY24 NDAA authority), allied stockpiling (Japan, Korea, EU, India, Australia), and a return to industrial-policy stockpile thinking. Sovereign demand is structurally large in critical minerals, defense, and selected biomanufacturing inputs.

**10. CO₂ and CDR (carbon dioxide removal) commodity market formation.** Direct air capture, biomass-to-storage, mineralization, ocean alkalinity — the CDR market is forming with hyperscaler purchases (Microsoft, Frontier coalition) anchoring early demand. CDR credits are converging on Power-Commodity-style trading structure with attribution-by-method, durability scoring, and emerging clearing infrastructure.

## 9. Open Questions for the Framework Rewrite

To resolve as the framework matures:

- **Where exactly is the boundary between Power Industries and Power Commodities?** When a battery factory sells cells, the framework says the factory is Power Industries and the cells are Power Commodities. But when an integrated EV maker uses captive cells, no commodity transaction occurs — the cells are internal transfers. The framework should specify the treatment of captive output versus market output.
- **Should compute-commodity formation be an explicit Cantus initiative?** The compute commodity is closely tied to Cantus's Compute-vector tenant base. Cantus could either participate passively (advisory + intelligence) or actively (standard-setting, structured offtake origination, possibly a desk in time). The framework should take a position on the activity level.
- **Treatment of green attribute commodities (RECs, hydrogen certificates, CDR credits) in Cantus campus underwriting.** These commodities affect tenant economics through IRA credit qualification, customer sustainability commitments, and increasingly capital-partner mandate compliance. The framework should specify whether Cantus offers attribution-structuring as part of the Industries advisory or as part of Commodities structuring.
- **Sovereign-stockpile counterparty class.** The US National Defense Stockpile, Saudi PIF mineral holdings, Australian Future Fund, and similar sovereign vehicles are increasingly active in Power Commodities. The framework should specify whether sovereign stockpiles are a Capital sub-industry function (matching Cantus capital partners) or a Commodities sub-industry function (matching Cantus structuring activity).
- **Cantus's posture on commodity-linked principal positions.** The arm is articulated as advise-and-structure, not principal. There are isolated cases (compute futures origination at scale, captive battery commodity for tenant aggregation) where principal capital might be justified. The framework should specify the threshold criteria.
