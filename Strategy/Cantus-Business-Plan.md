# The Cantus Business Plan

*v2 canonical operating plan. Anchored to [[Strategy/Cantus-Power-Sector-Thesis]] as the foundational articulation. Operationalizes the four-vertical architecture plus two Foundations into a multi-phase plan with specific Year 1–2 milestones, capital architecture, revenue model, organizational structure, and competitive position. Internal first; capital-partner-readable.*

## I. Executive Summary

Cantus is the integrator of the emerging Power sector — the firm that assembles Land, builds Facilities, powers them with integrated Infrastructure, makes them productive through Machines and software, and commercializes the integrated platform through capital, customers, contracts, and commodities. The firm operates through a four-vertical architecture (Development, Infrastructure, Technology, Markets) anchored by two cross-cutting Foundations (Labor/Community, CII/DealOS), evolving along the identity arc from Power Producer through Power Production Company to Power Studio over a multi-decade horizon.

The plan operates against the five-year formative window (2026–2031) during which Power consolidates as a recognized sector, and the longer compounding arc through 2036 and beyond. The Year 6 financial anchor is $100M in central-estimate free cash flow against an integrated platform with $5–8B in assets under management (direct + co-developed), $1.5–3B in committed fund capital, 6–8 operating Intelligent Campuses, and 80–150 team members. The Year 10 target is $300–500M FCF; Year 15 institutional scale of $750M–$1B FCF.

The firm is currently in the Building phase (Years 1–2) — pre-revenue, pre-fund, operating on balance sheet with active deal pipeline across multiple ERCOT projects (Mathis McDonald, GoodPeak, Project Joule, Palangana, Wilmer, T1 Energy, Singleton). The Activation phase (Years 3–4) commences with Fund I closing at $250M–$500M and the first Cantus-led Intelligent Campuses delivering. The Compounding phase (Years 5–7) scales the platform to multi-campus, multi-fund operations. The Mature platform phase (Years 8+) institutionalizes the Power Studio form.

The strategic claim is that no single-tier operator — generalist infrastructure fund, vertically integrated supply-side operator, master-planned industrial developer, BTS data center developer, or hyperscaler self-orchestration team — can replicate the integrated platform Cantus is constructing. The integration is the moat; the scale follows from the integration.

## II. Strategic Position

The firm's strategic position is fully articulated in [[Strategy/Cantus-Power-Sector-Thesis]]. The summary here:

Six economic forces — AI compute, reindustrialization, electrification, defense modernization, advanced manufacturing, energy transition — are converging on a single constraint: abundant, reliable, affordable, strategically controlled electricity. The convergence point is electricity; the value chain that converts electricity into industrial output is what becomes the Power sector. Power is structurally equivalent in this cycle to what Information Technology was in the cycle that began in the 1990s — a sector forming in real time out of activity currently fragmented across Real Estate, Utilities, Industrials, and Information Technology.

The Power sector comprises ten distinct sub-industries: Power Assets, Power Equipment, Power Services, Power Land, Power Facilities, Power Machines, Power Industries, Power Commodities, Power Markets (broad allocation), and Power Technology. Each has its own dedicated capital, regulatory, supply-chain, and counterparty dynamics. Each is currently treated by capital markets as a sub-segment of an adjacent sector. The integration across them is the structural opportunity.

The frontier company is becoming selectively asset-heavy. Hyperscalers, neoclouds, NVIDIA, Tesla, SpaceX, Anduril, Palantir are moving vertically across the Power stack because control of power, machines, facilities, and software determines strategic advantage. They are not pure-tech, pure-energy, pure-defense, or pure-manufacturing companies; they are Power-sector firms operating vertically. Cantus is constructed for the same reality from the integrator side rather than the operator side — owning the parts of the stack that determine competitive position, integrating the rest, and selling the integrated production to the demand-side operator.

## III. The Cantus Operating Architecture

Cantus operates through a **four-vertical architecture** that maps to value-creation stages rather than mirroring all ten sub-industries individually. The architecture is articulated at length in the four vertical pages ([[Strategy/Cantus-Development]], [[Strategy/Cantus-Infrastructure]], [[Strategy/Cantus-Technology]], [[Strategy/Cantus-Markets]]). The condensed sequence:

**Cantus Development creates the sites.** Direct principal in Power Land and Power Facilities. Assembles raw land, entitles it, builds Facilities. The Intelligent Campus is the product class.

**Cantus Infrastructure powers them.** Integrator + counterparty + structurer across Power Assets, Power Equipment, and Power Services. Cantus Prime is the firm-level orchestration label for the integrated supply-side product.

**Cantus Technology makes them productive.** Orchestrator across Power Machines and Power Technology for every Cantus campus; selective operator of principal businesses where the integration economics justify it. Cantus Compute is the named selective-operator business; Cantus Robotics is the modeled future addition; Cantus Edge sits inside this vertical.

**Cantus Markets monetizes and finances the output.** Operates across Power Industries (operator advisory), Power Commodities (output structuring), and Power Markets-broad (capital allocation, wholesale markets, retail bypass, commercial contracting). The vertical carries the firm's evolving identity as fund manager and Power Studio.

Beneath the four verticals, **two Foundations** cross-cut every vertical and compound at the firm level: Labor/Community (workforce, R1 universities, area education, IBEW JATCs, community engagement — substantively articulated in [[Strategy/Foundations/Labor-Community-Foundation]] and [[Strategy/Primary-Inputs-03-Labor-Community]]) and CII/DealOS (the firm's proprietary integration intelligence and deal-level operating system — substantively articulated in [[Strategy/Foundations/CII-DealOS-Foundation]], [[Products/CII-Iteration-Document]], and [[DealOS/README]]).

The strategic claim about customer relationship: Cantus does not compete with the operator on the operator's primary business. Cantus delivers the integrated production *around* the operator's industrial mission, letting the operator focus on its work while Cantus carries the integration across the other nine Power sub-industries.

## V. Geographic Strategy

**ERCOT is the primary current jurisdiction.** Texas concentration is consistent with where the active pipeline already operates (Mathis McDonald, GoodPeak, Joule, Palangana, Wilmer, T1, Singleton per [[_parameters]]). The strategic alignment: ERCOT load growth is the most acute in the US; Texas industrial policy aligns (no state income tax, Chapter 403 / HB 5 abatements, Texas Energy Fund at ~$10B for in-state dispatchable per [[_parameters]]); state regulatory framework is relatively permissive for private microgrid and behind-the-fence distribution; CenterPoint and ERCOT C-suite relationships through founding-partner network are operational.

**The expansion sequence (modeled):**

- **PJM** — Virginia (Loudoun and Mecklenburg counties as the AI compute concentration); the broader PJM-region capacity-and-transmission dynamics. Likely Year 3–4 extension as Activation phase deepens.
- **MISO** — Manufacturing and energy-transition density; Midwest reshoring corridors. Year 3–5 extension.
- **Western** (Arizona, Nevada, Utah primary) — semi and battery manufacturing concentration; water-rights dynamics; CAISO-adjacent. Year 4–6 extension.
- **Southeast** — Defense industrial base concentration; biopharma in NC and GA; Year 5+ extension.

**International** is explicitly out of scope for the strategic horizon documented here. The firm operates in the US during the formative window of US Power-sector emergence; international expansion is a Mature-platform-phase decision (Year 8+) and is not built into the operating plan.

## VI. Revenue Model

Revenue is generated across all four operating verticals through structurally distinct mechanisms:

### Cantus Development (Land + Facilities)

- **Development profit** on Cantus-led sites — price differential from raw to entitled to powered to built captured by Cantus as principal or JV partner. Reference: ~$2,500–$10,000/acre raw to ~$800K/acre powered in Texas per [[_parameters]].
- **Build-to-suit lease rent** on Cantus-developed Facilities — $80–120 per kW-month for hyperscale BTS leases under 15–20 year tenor.
- **Sale-leaseback / take-out** to capital partners (REITs, infra funds, sovereign wealth) at stabilized cap rates of 7–10% for hyperscale BTS, 5–7% for colo.
- **Ground lease income** on Cantus-owned Land where the tenant builds.
- **JV-deal economics** with Tier-1 MPC partners (Hillwood, Crow, Howard Hughes, Majestic) on co-developed campuses.

### Cantus Infrastructure (Assets + Equipment + Services)

- **Integration fees** on multi-EPC and multi-OEM orchestration for Cantus-led campuses.
- **Slot-trading economics** on reserved equipment manufacturing positions.
- **Selective principal positions** in Power Assets (BTM bridge equity, storage, substation, microgrid-controlled assets) — equity returns on operating infrastructure.
- **Cantus Prime branded product** revenue as the firm-level orchestration label.
- **O&M coordination fees** at campus operating lifetime.

### Cantus Technology (Machines + Power Technology)

- **Machine procurement coordination fees** for tenants.
- **Cantus Compute operating revenue** — hosted GPU capacity ($/MW deployed per month under multi-year contracts). Reference: $25–40M capex per MW deployed; revenue per MW depends on customer base scope (campus-internal-only vs. external).
- **Software integration revenue** — bundled into campus operating fees or sold separately as platform layer.
- **Cantus Robotics-as-a-Service** revenue (Compounding phase forward).

### Cantus Markets (Industries + Commodities + Markets-broad)

- **Advisory revenue** — activation fees ($500K–$2M on milestones per [[_parameters]]), retainers ($500K–$2M tenant-side / site-rep), carried interest (20–30% above 8% hurdle).
- **Fund management economics** — 1.0–1.75% management fee on committed capital; 15–20% carry above 8% hurdle. Year 6 modeled at $15–25M FCF contribution.
- **Co-development and advisory transaction economics** — $15–20M Year 6 contribution.
- **Information capability and adjacent commercial activity** — $10–15M Year 6 contribution.
- **Direct-PPA structuring fees** on hyperscale-scale arrangements.
- **Commodity offtake structuring fees** (Power Commodities sub-industry advisory).

## VII. Capital Architecture

The capital architecture runs through Cantus Markets vertical and follows a structured progression from balance-sheet operation to multi-fund platform:

**Years 1–2 (Building) — balance-sheet operation.** The firm operates on Dom and founding-partner balance sheet. Working capital, deal-pursuit costs, and first Land assembly positions are funded from founding capital. Advisory revenue and selective principal positions begin to generate cash. Capital-partner curation across the five Capital Partner Categories per [[_parameters]]: sovereign wealth, institutional infrastructure, strategic corporate LPs, family offices, and federal program capital.

**Years 3–4 (Activation) — Fund I.** Fund I closes at $250M–$500M target per [[_parameters]]. Vehicle structure to be specified: open-end vs. closed-end; LP-friendly fee model; specific carry hurdle (8% modeled); vintage strategy; co-investment policy. Fund I capital deployed primarily to Cantus Development (Land + Facility) principal positions, with selective allocation to Cantus Technology (Cantus Compute pilot) and Cantus Infrastructure (BTM bridge equity, storage).

**Years 5–7 (Compounding) — multi-fund family.** Subsequent funds modeled at $1.5–3B committed by Year 6 per [[_parameters]]. Potential vehicle structure: Cantus Development infrastructure fund (Land + Facility), Cantus Compute infrastructure fund (Power Machines hosted-compute), Cantus Infrastructure project fund (selective Power Assets principal positions), Cantus advisory carry vehicle. Strategic corporate LP architecture explored — Microsoft, Meta, Oracle, others wanting LP exposure to platforms they buy from.

**Years 8+ (Mature platform) — Power Studio.** Persistent capital vehicles, multi-fund family, controlled operating positions, institutionalized capital partner relationships. The Brookfield Infrastructure / BlackRock-GIP / Stonepeak / EQT / IFM / sovereign-wealth reference cohort, constructed from the demand-side outward.

**Federal program capital is modeled into standard deal architecture rather than treated as one-off.** DOE LPO (>$300B authorized), DPA Title III, Texas Energy Fund (~$10B), state EDC packages routed through Cantus Markets vertical at the transaction level.

## XI. Risk Factors and Mitigation

Cantus's risk profile is structurally distinct from a generalist infra fund or a standard real estate developer. Five primary risk categories carry through Building and Activation phases:

**Capital risk.** The firm operates on balance sheet through Building phase. The risk is that Fund I closing in Activation slips, delaying Cantus-led campus principal positions and stretching balance-sheet operation past sustainable horizon. *Mitigation:* multi-track capital partner curation across all five Capital Partner Categories per [[_parameters]]; advisory revenue scaling to fund operating expense independently of Fund I; selective strategic-corporate LP architecture explored as anchor commitment.

**Pipeline execution risk.** Active pipeline at ERCOT sites is structurally exposed to TSP and ERCOT process delays, interconnection queue dynamics under FERC Order 2023 implementation, and BTM bridge generation supply chain. *Mitigation:* CenterPoint and ERCOT C-suite engagement; BTM equipment slot reservations across multi-OEM cohort; Cantus Infrastructure orchestration across multiple bridge-generation arrangements per campus.

**Tenant risk.** Cantus campus underwriting depends on tenant covenant strength. Hyperscale tenants are IG-credit but operationally demanding; manufacturing tenants are more covenant-variable. The Cantus AI / Compute concentration creates correlation risk to hyperscaler capex cycle. *Mitigation:* multi-vector tenant strategy (six demand vectors per [[Strategy/Power-Sector/Power-Industries]]); IRA / CHIPS / DPA stack underwriting that captures industrial-policy-supported tenant economics; Cantus Markets advisory revenue independent of tenant principal positions.

**Workforce risk.** Skilled labor is the binding constraint on schedule. PWA compliance requirements amplify dependency on IBEW and trades councils. *Mitigation:* Labor/Community Foundation institutionalized in Building phase; R1 university partnerships feeding pipeline; multi-year apprenticeship cohort cultivation; PWA-compliance technology and tracking (open question on CII vs. Foundation owner).

**Regulatory and political risk.** Industrial policy is the largest single tailwind in the cycle; reversal of any major component (IRA tax credit framework, CHIPS Act administration, DPA Title III implementation) could materially affect Power Industries tenant economics. *Mitigation:* multi-credit-stack underwriting that does not depend on any single program; geographic diversification into multiple state incentive frameworks; engagement with federal program-capital agencies as institutional counterparties rather than transactional ones.

A sixth risk — **competitive risk** — exists but is structurally lower than the five above because the integration thesis explicitly distinguishes Cantus from each competitor class (see Section XII). The deeper risk in this category is that mega-vehicles (BlackRock-GIP, Brookfield, Stonepeak) institutionalize internal Cantus-equivalent capability before Cantus institutionalizes platform scale. *Mitigation:* speed of the formative-window execution (the five-year window 2026–2031); capital partner relationships that the mega-vehicles cannot duplicate; Cantus Compute and selected Cantus Technology operating businesses as defensible principal positions.
