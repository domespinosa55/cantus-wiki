# Cantus Market Sizing

**Subtitle:** Total Addressable Market, Serviceable Market, and Target Capture
**Classification:** Internal Strategy Document — foundation for investor materials
**Date:** May 2026

## The Two Demand Engines

Cantus serves two converging demand categories from a single integrated platform. Market sizing must capture both.

**Engine 1: AI Infrastructure & Data Centers** — hyperscale compute, colocation, GPU hosting, enterprise AI deployment.

**Engine 2: Advanced Manufacturing & Reindustrialization** — semiconductor fabs, battery gigafactories, EV manufacturing, defense/aerospace, critical minerals processing.

Both require the same physical inputs Cantus delivers: power-heavy industrial land and energy execution at scale. The sizing below quantifies each engine independently, then identifies the integrated opportunity Cantus is uniquely positioned to capture.

---

## Engine 1: AI Infrastructure & Data Center Market

### Demand Scale

U.S. data center power capacity is projected to grow from ~30 GW in 2025 to 90-110 GW by 2030 — a 3-4x expansion in five years. This represents 68% of total U.S. load growth over the period. ERCOT alone is tracking approximately 410 GW of large load interconnection requests as of March 2026, with ~87% from data centers — against a current Texas grid capacity of ~103 GW.

### Capital Deployment

- **2025:** U.S. data center construction spending reached ~$77.7B in starts (190% YoY increase). Total data center investment topped $425B.
- **2026:** Five companies alone (Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle) have announced $680-700B in combined capex, largely AI-related. ConstructConnect tracks 76 data center projects valued at $88B+ set to start in H1 2026 alone.
- **2026-2030:** Federal Reserve projects U.S. data center construction spending could reach $360-930B annually by late 2027.

### Market Revenue

U.S. data center market revenue: $81B (2025) → $145B (2030) at 12.4% CAGR. U.S. colocation segment: $40B (2025) → $72B (2030).

### Powered Land Premium

The binding constraint is power-ready sites. Land pricing reflects the scarcity:

- **Unpowered agricultural/industrial land near transmission:** $2,500-$10,000/acre
- **Powered land in Texas (infrastructure studies complete):** ~$800,000/acre
- **Powered land in primary markets (NoVA, Phoenix):** $1-4M+/acre
- **Average data center land transaction:** $244,000/acre, average parcel 244 acres (up 144% since 2022)

### Behind-the-Meter Generation

38% of data center facilities are expected to use onsite generation for primary power by 2030 (up from 13% in 2025). One in three hyperscalers/colocation providers now expects to operate entire campuses on onsite power. The U.S. data center power generators market: $1.6B (2025) → $3.0B (2032).

## Engine 2: Advanced Manufacturing & Reindustrialization

### Demand Scale

Since early 2025, more than $3 trillion in reshoring investments have been announced across all sectors. U.S. manufacturing construction spending tripled from $76B/month (annualized, 2021) to $230B (annualized, January 2025).

Within that, spending on factories for computers, electronics, and electrical equipment exploded 1,300% from $9B (2020) to $104B (2025) — driven by semiconductor plants and AI infrastructure equipment manufacturing.

### Semiconductor Fabs

$500B+ in private sector commitments to revitalize U.S. chipmaking, with $36B+ in CHIPS Act awards finalized. Major active projects:

- TSMC Arizona: 3-fab complex (2nm, 3nm, 4nm), $52-56B capex budget for 2026 alone
- Intel Fab 52: high-volume 18A (1.8nm) production operational
- Samsung Taylor, TX: expected operational 2026
- Multiple packaging facilities under construction

Each advanced fab requires 100-300+ MW of reliable power and costs $15-40B to build.

### Battery Gigafactories

~34 battery factories planned, under construction, or operational in the U.S. Major facilities coming online 2025-2026:

- Panasonic (De Soto, KS), Samsung SDI/Stellantis, SK/Ford BlueOval City (TN), Hyundai (GA), AESC (SC), Samsung SDI/GM (IN), BMW (SC)
- Large gigafactories consume ~2.4 GWh of electricity daily
- EV batteries and solar components account for 31% of all reshoring jobs; semiconductors another 35%

### Industrial Construction Pipeline

346M SF of industrial space under construction nationally. The pipeline has contracted 25% YoY as traditional spec development slows — but advanced manufacturing (semiconductors, defense, biomanufacturing) continues expanding. Power availability has become the single most critical constraint in industrial site selection, with 1-2 year delays now standard for large loads.

## TAM — Total Addressable Market

The TAM represents the total economic value of developing and operating power-heavy industrial campuses in the U.S. across both demand engines.

### Calculation Approach

**Data Center Development TAM:**
- New U.S. data center capacity needed: ~60-80 GW by 2030
- Average campus size: 100-500 MW → implies 150-600+ campus-scale projects
- Average development value per campus: $500M-$2B (land + power infrastructure + shell)
- **Data center development TAM: $150-300B through 2030** (cumulative)

**Advanced Manufacturing Development TAM:**
- U.S. manufacturing construction spending: ~$200B/year (2025), concentrated in power-heavy categories
- Power-heavy advanced manufacturing share (semis, batteries, defense, critical minerals): ~50% of spend
- Developer-addressable share (site development, shell, infrastructure vs. process equipment): ~30-40%
- **Advanced manufacturing development TAM: $30-40B/year, or $150-200B through 2030**

**Combined Development TAM: $300-500B through 2030**

### Energy Execution TAM (Overlay)

Behind-the-meter and bridge generation represents an additional revenue layer:

- 60-80 GW of new capacity × 30-40% requiring BTM/bridge power × $1,500-2,000/kW capex
- **Energy execution TAM: $30-60B through 2030** (equipment + services, not including ongoing energy revenue)

### Operated Capacity TAM (Long-Term)

GPU-as-a-service / compute hosting at Cantus-operated campuses. The GPU rental market alone is scaling rapidly (H100 1Y index up 40% YoY). This is a Years 5+ revenue layer and will be sized when operational data supports it.

## SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market

The SAM narrows to the segments Cantus can credibly serve given its geographic focus, operating model, and capability profile.

### Geographic Filter: ERCOT-First, Sun Belt Expansion

- **ERCOT (Texas):** 410 GW of large load requests. Even at 5-10% realization, that's 20-40 GW of actual deployment. Texas manufacturing construction: ~$30B+ annually.
- **Sun Belt expansion markets (Years 3-5):** Georgia, North Carolina, Arizona, Tennessee, Ohio — the states capturing the largest share of both data center and reshoring activity.
- **Geographic SAM multiplier:** ~35% of national TAM (Texas + Sun Belt = ~35% of U.S. data center capacity by 2030).

### Operating Model Filter

Cantus operates three models: developer/landlord, co-development, and operated capacity. The SAM includes:

- **Developer/landlord:** Large-campus multi-tenant projects (100-500+ MW). Realistic pipeline: 5-15 campuses through 2030.
- **Co-development:** Activating partner land (Hillwood, Crow, Howard Hughes, etc.). 10-30 partnerships through 2030.
- **Advisory/coordination:** Fee-based site advisory for tenants and developers who don't use Cantus as principal developer. Broadest reach.

### SAM Calculation

| Segment | SAM (Through 2030) | Basis |
|---------|-------------------|-------|
| Data center campus development (TX + Sun Belt) | $50-100B | 35% of $150-300B development TAM |
| Advanced manufacturing campus development | $15-25B | TX/Sun Belt share of $150-200B |
| Energy execution (BTM/bridge) | $10-20B | Geographic overlap with campus development |
| Advisory and coordination fees | $500M-$1B | Fee revenue on deal flow not developed directly |
| **Total SAM** | **$75-145B** | |

## SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market (Years 1-7)

The SOM represents what Cantus can realistically capture in its first seven years of operation. This is what the financial model should be built against.

### Year 1-2: Foundation (Advisory + First Co-Development)

- 3-5 advisory/coordination mandates at $500K-$2M each: **$2-8M**
- 1-2 co-development activations with fee + promote: **$3-10M** (fees; promote on backend)
- Equipment advisory/procurement margin: **$1-3M**
- **Year 1-2 SOM: $6-21M revenue**

### Year 3-4: Platform Scale (First Campuses + Expanded Co-Dev)

- 2-4 Cantus-led campus developments (200-500 MW each): **$200-600M project value**
- Developer margin (15-25% of development cost): **$30-150M**
- 5-10 co-development partnerships generating fees + carry: **$10-30M** (fees; carry accrues)
- Energy execution revenue (BTM installation + services): **$20-50M**
- Advisory revenue continuing: **$5-15M**
- **Year 3-4 SOM: $65-245M revenue**

### Year 5-7: Compound (Multiple Campuses + Operated Capacity + Fund Revenue)

- 5-10 operating campuses generating lease revenue: **$100-300M/year**
- Co-development portfolio at scale: **$20-50M/year** (fees + realized carry)
- Energy services and ancillary revenue: **$30-80M/year**
- Fund management fees (1.5-2% on $500M-$1B AUM): **$7.5-20M/year**
- First operated capacity revenue (Edge/Compute): **$20-100M/year**
- **Year 5-7 SOM: $180-550M/year**

### Enterprise Value Implications

At maturity (Year 7+), the platform generates revenue across four streams: development margin, lease/capacity revenue, fund management fees, and operated capacity. Comparable platforms trade at:

- Infrastructure asset managers (Brookfield model): 15-25x fee-related earnings
- Infrastructure operators/developers (Hines model): 8-15x EBITDA
- Data center REITs: 20-30x FFO

Cantus's integrated model — combining fee streams, asset ownership, and operated capacity — would likely command a premium to any single-category comp.

## Key Assumptions and Sensitivities

| Assumption                                      | Base Case | Downside | Upside |
| ----------------------------------------------- | --------- | -------- | ------ |
| U.S. data center capacity addition through 2030 | 60 GW     | 35 GW    | 90 GW  |
| ERCOT large load realization rate               | 7%        | 3%       | 12%    |
| BTM/onsite power adoption rate                  | 35%       | 20%      | 50%    |
| Manufacturing construction CAGR (2026-2030)     | 5%        | -5%      | 12%    |
| Cantus campus development count (Years 1-7)     | 8         | 3        | 15     |
| Average campus power capacity                   | 300 MW    | 150 MW   | 500 MW |
| Co-development partnerships (Years 1-7)         | 15        | 5        | 30     |

### What Could Go Wrong

- **AI demand plateau:** Training compute scaling laws break; inference efficiency gains outpace demand. Mitigant: advanced manufacturing demand is independent of AI cycle.
- **Power supply relief:** Utility interconnection timelines compress, eliminating BTM premium. Mitigant: structural grid underinvestment takes decades to correct.
- **Capital market dislocation:** Rising rates compress development margins and slow fund formation. Mitigant: advisory and co-development models are capital-light.
- **Regulatory risk:** ERCOT/PUCT rule changes disadvantage large loads or BTM generation. Mitigant: deep regulatory expertise, grid-supportive project design.
- **Competition:** Established developers (QTS, Compass, Stack) integrate energy execution capability. Mitigant: manufacturing vertical + co-development model + community embeddedness are structural differentiators they haven't built.

## The Integration Premium

No existing market participant spans both demand engines with integrated energy execution:

| Competitor Category | Data Center | Manufacturing | Energy Execution | Integrated Campus |
|-------------------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| DC developers (QTS, Compass, Stack) | Deep | None | Limited bridge | Single-vertical |
| Industrial developers (Hillwood, Crow, Prologis) | Entering | Traditional | None | No power capability |
| IPPs (Vistra, NRG, Constellation) | Partnering | None | Deep | Not developers |
| Hyperscalers (self-build) | Internal | N/A | Building | Captive, not platform |
| **Cantus** | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** |

The market sizing above treats the two engines independently. The integration premium — multi-tenant campuses where hyperscale anchor demand subsidizes globally competitive manufacturing power pricing — is additive and unique to Cantus. No existing market report sizes this because no one is doing it yet.
